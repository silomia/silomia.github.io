“If-” you'll be a Man, my son

Famous poem by Rudyard Kipling, written in 1909.

“If-” you'll be a Man, my son

If you can keep your head when all about you
Are losing theirs and blaming it on you,
If you can trust yourself when all men doubt you.
But make allowance for their doubting too;
If you can wait and not be tired by waiting.
Or being lied about, don't deal in lies,
Or being hated, don't give way to hating,
And yet don't look too good, nor talk too wise:

If you can dream —and not make dreams your master
If you can think —and not make thoughts your aim
If you can meet Triumph and Disaster
And treat those two impostors just the same;
If you can bear to hear the truth you've spoken
Twisted by knaves to make a trap for fools.
Or watch the things you gave your life to broken,
And stoop and build'em up with worn-out tools:

If you can make one heap of all your winnings
And risk it on one turn of pitch-and-toss,
And lose, and start again at your beginnings
And never breathe a word about your loss;
If you can force your heart and nerve and sinew
To serve your turn long after they are gone,
And so hold on when there is nothing in you
Except the Will which says to them: “Hold on!”

If you can talk with crowds and keep your virtue,
Or walk with Kings —nor lose the common touch,
If neither foes nor loving friends can hurt you,
If all men count with you, but none too much;
If you can fill the unforgiving minute,
With sixty seconds' worth of distance run.
Yours is the Earth and everything that's in it,
And —which is more— you'll be a Man, my son!

Comment

Here is the famous poem “If-” by Rudyard Kipling (1909). This text is not written by a man, but it was inspired to a man.

There are sometimes words that ring true because they make vibrate our soul and our heart, as well as awaken the sublime and noble human being that lies dormant in us.

This poem is famous, it has been influential to so many readers. Some memorize only one sentence, others use it as a guide for their life. You may have heard of it before, but have you actually read it?

Take the poem quatrain by quatrain and ask yourself if this is your concern, are you like this, can you make it better? Read it and feel it. I am sure you will learn something from it and it will help you evolve.

In order to understand this text today, it is necessary to see the poem in context. Written at the very beginning of the 20th century, it celebrates the virtues of stoicism in the British Victorian era when it was noble to restrain one's emotions and dominate one's passions. This society distinguished the strong man, fearless and hardened, from the weak man, slave to his feelings.

Today, my interpretation of this poem is different. There is no longer any reference to stoicism. We are sentient beings, we feel pleasure and pain. It is then respectable to express our emotions and sorrows. However, the text calls for the courage of the one who is undergoing hardship. Although we do not choose the happening of a painful event, misfortune or situation, we choose how to react to it instead of feeling misery and self-pity. Even if he is brought down by fate, man must rise up to become sovereign over his life again. That is the message I learn.

And if you are passionate about this poem, I invite you to read the story of Dr Leander Starr Jameson (and this paper from the Daily Mail), adventurer and friend of Rudyard Kipling, who inspired the writing of this legendary work.
